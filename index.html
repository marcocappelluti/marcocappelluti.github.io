02 / Research

My research examines how different types of economic shocks drive support for far-right parties across Europe.

PhD Thesis

Dimensions of Discontent: Essays on Economic Insecurity and Far-Right Support in Post-Financial Crisis Europe

University College London, 2026

Published American Journal of Political Science, 2024

Geographies of Discontent: Public Service Deprivation and the Rise of the Far Right in Italy

with Simone Cremaschi, Paula Rettl, and Catherine De Vries

Best Article Award, AJPS 2025

Coverage: New York Times, Financial Times, The Guardian, VoxEU, Brookings. Cited by UN Special Rapporteur on Extreme Poverty report to UN General Assembly (2025).

Does state withdrawal from public service provision fuel support for far-right parties? We argue that geographic disparities in access to public services generate localized grievances that anti-establishment parties can exploit. Combining granular administrative data on health care and school closures in Italy with electoral returns, we show that municipalities experiencing sharper declines in public service access became significantly more likely to support far-right parties. The effect is not explained by economic deprivation or population decline alone, pointing to a distinct political pathway from service deprivation to far-right support.

Uncertainty, Nativism, and the Politics of Withdrawal: Labor Market Insecurity and Far-Right Support in Europe

Marco Cappelluti

Why does labor market insecurity translate into far-right voting for some workers but not others? This paper argues that the politicization of economic risk depends on the interaction between objective exposure to labor market uncertainty and subjective perceptions of competitive threat from immigration. Using individual-level panel data from European Social Surveys linked to regional labor market indicators, I show that nativist attitudes amplify the electoral consequences of insecurity, while universalistic welfare commitments attenuate them. The findings illuminate the conditional political economy of far-right support in post-crisis Europe.

Working Paper

Democracy Without Delivery: Public Service Retrenchment, Anti-Democratic Discontent and Far-Right Support in Italy

Marco Cappelluti

This paper investigates whether public service retrenchment erodes democratic legitimacy and channels political discontent toward anti-democratic far-right parties. Leveraging spatial and temporal variation in service closures across Italian municipalities, I show that retrenchment increases both anti-democratic attitudes and far-right vote shares, with the two outcomes strongly mediated by perceptions of governmental unresponsiveness. The results suggest that the crisis of democratic delivery is a crucial — and underappreciated — link between austerity and democratic backsliding.

Working Paper

Blame and Backlash: Austerity, Euroscepticism and Far-Right Support in Europe

Marco Cappelluti

This paper examines how fiscal austerity shapes Euroscepticism and far-right support across European countries. I argue that the political consequences of austerity depend critically on whether voters attribute fiscal retrenchment to domestic governments or to European institutions. When EU-level conditionality is salient, austerity increases Euroscepticism and boosts far-right parties that combine economic nationalism with anti-EU rhetoric. Cross-national evidence from the post-financial-crisis period supports this blame-attribution mechanism.

03 / About

I am a PhD candidate in Political Science at University College London, affiliated with the Political Economy Cluster, supervised by Lucy Barnes, Tim Hicks, and Eleanor Woodhouse. My research examines how different types of economic shocks — labor market insecurity, fiscal austerity, and public service retrenchment — shape far-right support and Euroscepticism across Europe.

Before UCL, I was a Pre-Doctoral Research Fellow at the Dondena Centre and a Research Intern at the LEAP Centre, Bocconi University. I hold an MSc in Politics and Policy Analysis (cum laude) from Bocconi and a BSc in Economics and Social Sciences, also from Bocconi. I was a visiting student at the Hertie School of Governance in Berlin.
